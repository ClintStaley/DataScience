CSC 344

Data Science 1

Instructor

  • Clint Staley, Andy Crump, or external visitor

Catalog Description

Theoretical and practical aspects of the analysis and display of very large datasets, including data types and data cleaning, classic statistical methods like correlation, unsupervised learning methods like K-means and other clustering, itemset analysis, sequence analysis, Bayesian analysis and other methods. Students will write code for analysis and display.

Prerequisite(s) C or better in CSCI 171, 182, 220, and 240. Math 273 and 366.

See the Course Bibliography. I will assign readings from selected items, the content of which you must understand and may be tested on. Other items there are for reference.

Course Goals

  1. Understand intuitively the mathematics underlying the algorithms for pattern recognition in large data sets.

  2. Understand the organizational and logistical problems associated with high volume data, and the methods for solving them.

  3. Write code to clean organize and analyze large datasets for significant patterns, using a variety of algorithms and methods.

  4. Understand data display methods, and write code to display large datasets

Weekly Topics

  • (1) Data types and data cleaning

  • (1) Itemset analysis

  • (1) K-means and unsupervised clustering

  • (1) Naive Bayes

  • (2) Linear, multiple, logistic regression

  • (2) Sequence Mining

  • (1) Classification and Linear discriminant analysis

  • (3) Data display and visualization

  • (2) Selected other topics

Course Instruction

In-Lecture Questions

I often ask questions in lecture to help you think through the issues being discussed. For this highly-conceptual class, we'll make that process a little more formal. Once or twice per lecture I'll ask a question for you to consider, and to write down answers to. We'll ask for answers in class, and I'll collect answers on paper. I'll randomly score one of them every week or so. Each such scored answer will be worth 1-2% of the final grade.

Labs and Project

We'll have homeworks, and small labs throughout the semester. "Labs" will be small coding or computational projects to explore and reinforce concepts from reading and lecture. They will be important preparation for the midterms.

Once you have lab experience with various data analysis and display methods, you'll do a larger project toward the end of the semester.

Midterms

Class midterms will be partially on-paper tests of concepts and vocabulary. But at least the first one or two will also include a practical exercise to complete as a significant part of the test, generally based on the most recent lab. This is a little experimental, and depending on its success I may run up to 4 such midterms, roughly each 3 weeks. The 50 points for midterms will be divided accordingly. 

Individual Work Required

Collaboration on code is strictly forbidden in this course.  Each individual must do his/her own work on the programming projects, and work only with their partners in team projects.  Violation of this standard results in an automatic F in the course, plus additional penalties.

I'll hand out in class an agreement to sign indicating that you understand and will adhere to this policy.  You must get me a signed copy of this agreement by the end of the first week of class or I will drop you from the course.
